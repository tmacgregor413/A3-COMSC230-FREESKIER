THE CLIMB

Skiing itself has it challenges. Whether you are new or a professional, there is always one step further than where you are now. A big thing about your experience while skiing, has to do with the terrain. Some mountains are made for beginners, some for intermediate, and some for the most daring skiers out there. It is only up to your courage and perservearence on whether or not you push yourself to that next step.

BEGINNER MOUNTAINS

There around 500-600 ski mountains in the USA. That's not to say that there are other mountains that aren't easier than others that are not mentioned. If you are just beginning to learn how to ski, depending on the mountain, you will mostl likely stay on green trails or some easy blue trails. Mountains with a wide selection of these types of trails ensure that you get comfy on different types of terrain while keeping the difficulty down. Here of some of the most popular beginner mountains in the Intermountain Region.

Steamboat Mountain, Colorado --> 165 trails, 14% of which are green trails. Rated 4/5 Stars for beginners.

Aspen Moutnain, Colorado --> 335 runs, 15.5% of which are green trails. Rated 4/5 Stars for beginners.

Breckenridge Mounatain, Colorado --> 2,908 skiable acres, 14% of which are green trails. Rated 4.5/5 Stars for beginners.

Beaver Creek Mountain, Colorado --> 1,832 skiable acres, 19% of which are green trails. Rated 5/5 Stars for beginners.

EXPERT MOUNTAINS

Most mountains have mostly advanced trails. Depending on where you go, black and double black diamond trails will be different. Here are some of the most popular advanced mountains in North America.

Jackson Hole, Wyoming --> 116 runs, 50% of which are advanced trails. Rated 5/5 Stars for advanced skiers.

Jasper Moutnain, Alberta --> 1,675 skiable acres, 40% of which are advanced trails. Rated 5/5 Stars for advanced skiers.
